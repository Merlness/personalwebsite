I've never been good at writing. In fact, I think I've only written for school. This might be my first journal entry ever — or at least, let's just say it is. My memory isn't the best.

Before I arrived, I had mixed feelings wrapped in a nonchalant front. A blasé attitude masking what was underneath: excitement and nervousness tangled together. I'm a 28-year-old man — I should be able to do anything. But the only thing I could compare this to was camping and working with horses.

Still, I'm used to new. I enjoy novelty. And life is what you make it, so fuck it. Let's enjoy what we can out of life. I only have so much time — let me learn and try new things.

## Landing in Anchorage

![Mountain landscape](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC05913.jpg)

My first new memories begin when I land in Anchorage. My flight was delayed, so I had extra time. I ate a reindeer hot dog — there might be a mild difference, I can't really tell. I walked to one of the wings of the airport where I didn't have to go through inspection to get to my connecting flight.

The plane held maybe 30 people. From outside the gate, you could see this small aircraft framed against beautiful mountains, snow covering their peaks. It was very picturesque.

![Tidal flats and waterways](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC05927.jpg)

As we flew through the air, I got my first glimpse of what the landscape looks like. If Minnesota is the land of 10,000 lakes, then Alaska is the land of 10 million ponds at least. Near the ocean, everything seemed like a mixture of land and water — marshes or swamps, maybe. But what do I know? All I saw were little water pockets everywhere.

The tide shift is immense. From the plane, I couldn't tell where the ocean began and what was mud, or where the shoreline was — if it even exists, since it changes so much. But from above you could see the fractals in the mud, these little crevasses forming the same pattern over and over again.

![Coastal patterns](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC05942.jpg)

## Trial by Fire

I landed and tried to find my crew. Since my flight was delayed, I had a trial by fire. We rushed to the locker where all the boats were and where the crew had slept while setting everything up. Where they slept looked like a refugee area, inside a shipping container. I'm glad I arrived days later.

They dropped me off to get my fishing license — I met someone from Tucson — then I had to run back to the locker. We packed the truck with supplies and rushed to the docks where our boats were. We had to pack everything and get to the other side of the bay.

![Boats and bay](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC05969.jpg)

As we were about to head off, we had to wait for a large drift net fishing boat to get in the water. It was fairly impressive in size. The bay is fairly large — it takes about an hour to cross — and the crew seemed friendly and nice. I was the only new person. Since this was all so foreign to me, I didn't even ask questions, just did what I was told.

## The Point

We arrived at our point. We unloaded and brought our stuff to the "shack." There I met the rest of the crew. There was no time for chitchat. We began fixing and setting everything up.

There's a waterfall full of melted ice or glacier water that runs down near our "town" of maybe 100 fisher humans. A rubber line provides water for everyone from this waterfall. We made sure the water got to all the sinks and showers and everything we needed. We organized the food — which was substantial, since everything is bought beforehand and shipped here, as everything is expensive in the middle of nowhere. We organized dishes, swept, and tried to make our temporary home back from nature.

![The camp](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC06048.jpg)

Small problems kept popping up. The shower pump wasn't working. The copper gas pipe was leaking. Thankfully, nothing huge. We made food around 10 PM and tried to sleep at midnight.

Midnight is just a word that shouldn't be taken literally. I remember the few days I spent in Iceland, where there was what some people would call light from about 11 AM till around 2:30 PM, then darkness around 3 PM. Once we saw the sun for 30 minutes in 5 days. This, of course, is summer, so it's reversed. Although I've seen short glimpses of the sun here, it's light most of the time. By midnight it was still light enough to walk around without needing a headlamp.

The night was cold. I should have bought an expensive sleeping bag.
