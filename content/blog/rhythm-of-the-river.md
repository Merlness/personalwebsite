I think I wrote last in the calm before the storm. It's been around 5 days since I began the pre-season, and Jesus Christ, these people are strong. It's not necessarily the most incredible workout — it's that it's constant.

## The Opener

Let's take yesterday, for example. We began at 9 PM. We set nets, picked them, pulled them through the boat, took them to the tenders, then back to the cabin. We got to the cabin around 12:30 AM, ate, and got back out there till around 3 AM. Then we woke up at 11-ish to go to the boats and begin the whole process over again.

It's usually a 17-hour window to fish, allowed by Fish and Game and our fishery, Peter Pan. During that time, the beginning — the "openers" — is the most important. After that, you do the usual: setting, pulling fish, and resetting. Then you take a break for food or coffee or a short nap, and get back out there till it closes. And the cycle continues.

![Working on the water](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC06126.jpg)

## Being New

You ever been new at something? It's a bittersweet thing.

The downsides are your ignorance and your trying not to fuck up. Having someone explain things to you at a basic level — although they're not condescending — makes you feel small, like a child listening to what the grownups have to say and going along. And there are the mistakes that can be made, especially out here where profit and safety are involved. You're constantly checking the few things you learned, repeating them to yourself, which makes you mentally and physically tired.

But I'm not trying to be negative. The good things about being new: it expands your perception. Learning in general helps you learn better later because you can relate it to something. It makes you feel young. It's always rough to be stagnant. It prevents time from just slipping away.

There's generally a sense of accomplishment. When you start recognizing patterns and being able to do things without being reminded — that's a huge step. Plus, it's more enjoyable doing rather than knowing.

![Weather on the bay](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC06142.jpg)

## The First Days

I woke up the next day. It was more of the same. We fixed those little things we needed to be comfortable. We unloaded the rest of the boat's valuables — mostly food and other materials we needed to survive comfortably in this cold and wet place.

The rain came and went. Nothing too harsh yet, but enough to make you conscious of it. I do love the pitter-patter from the roof. It's nice because it's a break from the mosquitoes trying to swarm you. The bad thing is it creates more still water for the larvae to hatch.

We fixed and organized the cabin. Side note: "Swamp" is the name of the cabin most of the guys sleep in. Creative!

![The boats](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC06152.jpg)

Afterwards, I got my first nibble of what this upcoming summer brings. We suited up — which means sweatpants, normal underwear, a T-shirt, a sweater, a hat. This is my regular uniform now. Then we put on the suit: neoprene chest-high waders that keep me waterproof, gloves, and a rain jacket.

Today we just got the nets ready and did some minor things out on the boats — turned them, moved them. But to me, this was still all an adventure. And so far, I enjoy this type of weather. I'm not used to it, and honestly, this shit all feels manly.

I have all my suit on, so I'm fairly dry except for my face. Whether we're on the shore or the river, I feel the small rain droplets hit my face and the cool ocean breeze pass through my beard. It feels marvelous. Like I'm doing something right, something useful.

We return. The same routine happens: clean, make food, tell jokes, grab a beer, go to bed.

![Misty morning on the water](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC06201.jpg)

## Preparation

I was given an extra blanket, so I slept well this time. I think I overslept because when I woke up, we had to run and get suited up. I put on this clumpy gear and got back out onto the water.

Same feeling of fulfillment. We got to jump onto boats from the shore, or from boat to boat — all very fun. In the end, we took the boats out to the deep end because when the time comes to actually go fishing, the tide will be low and we can walk through the mud to get there.

![Deep water](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC06210.jpg)

It's currently the period between our preparation and go time. Our bellies have been filled and we're supposed to be relaxing because we may — most likely will — be out fishing for 17 hours.

Our time to fish is coordinated by the fishery we work for and the Fish and Game commission. I'm still extremely ignorant of what's going on. The guys have been doing a good job to help ease me in. There are many things they say to try to prepare me, and hopefully I'll know what they mean when the time comes.

I'll try to rest and do what I can to prepare for the storm.
