Today is the Fourth of July, and the sun came out unexpectedly. I haven't been able to write for a bit, and last time I was interrupted. I hope most of the nuances are still fresh, but for at least right now, I want to write and remember three memories that may have happened in a 24-hour span — a truly beautiful day.

## The Wild West

To set the scene, I have to mention the days before. We had our biggest day of the season so far. We were nine strong, and we mainly fish on an estuary. It's large — from the town across the bay to the point where I am, it takes about 30 minutes in a skiff to cross over.

Sometimes, due to several factors, there's a river nearby that's much smaller and is open for business on rare occasions. What makes this special is that there can be many fish or no fish — it's like a gamble to go there. From the point where we are, it's about an hour's ride to get there, so it's not convenient.

![River approach](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC06228.jpg)

Also, on the Nushagak, there are set physical locations where each skiff is registered in a very orderly fashion. If you go in the wrong place, there's a big fine. In the Wood River, it's said to be the Wild West — anyone can take any spot. I've heard rumors of people even using weapons to claim specific locations.

This is important because on this day, the Wood River was opened for the first time this season. Since it's the Wild West, it was in our best interest to be there — all three skiffs strong. Although we went a few weeks earlier and set signs to "claim our sites," anyone could have easily removed them and been justified. So we all had to go to be united and strong in order to claim our locations.

We couldn't stay at our place on the point because it was so far away. We had to stay in Dillingham across the bay, which meant gathering our things and moving them through the mud into the boats. Imagine gathering your electronics and sleeping supplies and clothes and everything important on your back, through mud that when you step on it usually sinks to your ankles and sometimes to your knees. It's pretty tough.

We did this because we might need to sleep in a locker for a few days if the fish were on the Wood.

![Mudflats at low tide](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC06278.jpg)

As we were traveling to the river, it began to rain. The day was long and hard and cold. We barely had a few fish, but we claimed our sites and were able to go back to Dillingham and shower and rest for the next few hours before our next opener on the Nushagak.

## The Big Day

The next day was warm on the Nushagak. I woke up at 6 AM to set with some of the crew. We came back for coffee and then went to pick the fish with everyone. This had been the largest day of the year so far. There was a longer day where we had to fish through the night, but this was a huge day, and it was very lucky that we had nine people.

It was a wonderful day to fish — nice and warm and not too sunny. I came back at about 11 PM. During that time, in our lone boat, we picked a little less than 20,000 pounds of fish. I think for our three boats we got close to 40,000 pounds.

![Full nets](/assets/portfolio/Alaska/DSC06289.jpg)

There's nothing much to say about this except we were constantly doing something: picking fish, moving the net, delivering — rinse and repeat all day.

Throughout the night I kept waking up because my forearms kept going numb. They've been worked so hard. This has been happening nightly since the first big day, actually. It's such a weird feeling.

## Fortune's Wheel

Each day has its nuances and memories. Those were interesting, but they're in the "hard" category, which makes the next moments I'll tell you about special.

Usually when Lady Fortune spins the wheel, we always expect it to land on good fortune and are disappointed when it lands on bad. Good fortune seems deceiving because we expect it to be the norm, but in reality, it's the bad fortune that's good for us — it reminds us of the good and is more honest in a way.

I don't know how long I'll have this mentality, but so far it seems nice to take the good and the bad with a stoic attitude because they can both be flipped depending on the situation.

## Steam Rising from the Mud

The beginning and end of this day were spectacular — things you keep with you for a lifetime.

The morning after the long day, our boats were dry. This means we anchored them too close in, and the tide went away, so we were stuck in the mud or sand and couldn't reach the water.

I was fortunate because it was another sunny, rare day. So much so that the sun heated the mud and fog began to rise. This is a beautiful and unusual sight. Steam from the mud has a surreal effect to it.

I'd seen it before, but on this day we were out toward the sand, which is past the mud on the low tide, and there was wind. This means I could sit on the sand and watch this steam flow through me.

This was an incredible experience. Imagine seeing the dark sand with ghosts flying over it from a far direction, then flowing through you with a cool breeze that your senses long for. This happened constantly for about 10 minutes until water reached our boats and we were able to begin fishing.

## Sunset on the River

This day was above average for fishing — we did well. It was after that stands out.

This was the last night we would fish as nine, for two had to return. Since it was sunny, the sunset was marvelous — many shades of blue for the faraway mountains and orange from the sunlight.

As the sun was setting, we put the three boats together, played some chill music, and drank a beer.

It reminds me of the scene in *Shawshank Redemption* where Andy gets a beer for his pals, and for those moments, all is right with the world. Here, my whole worry is fish — nothing else. And after several long days, for us to unite and share in the camaraderie with a cold brew on the river, with some slow jams and the sky showing off her colors for us — this is the fulfillment most people search for.

Those are the moments one carries till the grave.

## Almost Overboard

Now that I was able to sit and contemplate on it, I can't beat what I wrote above — that was too beautiful of a moment. But this one is pretty dope.

The next morning I was still recovering from everything, really. Several times on the boat, you need to reach over the water to grab the net with a hook. Usually you can grab it and pull it on the boat, and we do this thing called rolling the net to grab the fish from it.

Sometimes the net can be heavy, or the boat is still moving and it gets too heavy. In this particular instance, I missed my first swing with the hook and had to shift my weight to reach the net the second time. This, mixed with the boat moving a bit too far over the net, made grabbing it and trying to pull the net over too hard.

I lost my balance and dropped the hook into the water because it was too heavy, and I was falling overboard. As I was falling, I was able to twist my body and grab onto the ledge of the boat with both hands. I held on as my body fell into the water. Since I had waders to my chest, I was able to stay dry. I then was able to shimmy myself to the side of the boat and climb back on.

That sequence — hooking and falling and catching — maybe took a second and a half, so it was all reactionary. I think picking fish and grabbing lines and anchors has greatly increased my grip strength, which was very useful in this situation.

Anyway, it was a pretty dope experience, and I got mad props from my peers.
